## Digitizing the *Calalogus Baronum* 

Ryan Alves

Binghamton University, Spring 2026 

For my digital history project, I digitized the first 99 entries of the medieval registry, the *Catalogus Baronum*. The catalog was created in the 12th-century kingdom of Sicily to keep track of the lords along with their fiefs and their military obligations to the crown. These obligations were articulated in terms of knights and soldiers and tied to fiefs. Using the critical edition of the text, from Evelyn Jamison, along with Google Copilot, AI, Python and Leaflet.js, I translate and transcribe the Latin within into a series of easy-to-read and searchable tables. Alongside this table is an interactive choropleth map that allows users to project the fief(s) of a lord, highlighting the modern geographical location of the fief and the obligation associated with them. The database and map help medievalists better understand the spatial distribution of power and resources within the kingdom of Sicily, which is a question difficult to answer due to the complexity of the kingdom’s bureaucracy. Although the database is only 1/16th of the entire catalog, the data suggests a concentration of wealth around the communes that are now called Andria and Bitonto. Currently, there are no other sites or programs that allow historians to visualize the Catalogus Baronum or access the information without knowing Latin or possessing the rare critical edition, making the project novel.
