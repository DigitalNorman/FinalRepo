### Methodology/ AI usage/ Research question

The Catalogus Baronum, or Catalog of Barons, is a Latin register that records the military obligation owed by the lords within the twelfth-century Kingdom of Sicily. The register was created under the first king, Roger II, in the twelfth century and expanded during the thirteenth. The registry contains over a thousand entries that include the names of lords, the sum of their obligations and where their fiefs were located. These fees were articulated as knights and soldiers and were managed by either a constable or a count. This project and website seek to make accessing some of these entries easier.


The Catalogus Baronum was written entirely in Latin and currently lacks a database of any sort. One would need to translate and organize hundreds of entries to meaningfully use the register. Though difficult to access, the document provides information regarding the lordship and royal administration of the mainland kingdom. The ability to access this information more easily allows scholars or interested internetgoers, who lack the extensive training required, to meaningfully interpret and contribute to a large conversation about the kingdom of Sicily. Using the modern critical edition by Evelyn Jamison, this project does just that and records the first 99 entries into a searchable database (these locations are organized by modern municipality boundaries, with provided contemporary names). Accompanying the database is a corresponding map that allows users to project and compare entries in real-time. The website was created to answer a few questions, mainly which municipalities/villages owed the most fees to the crown. Although there are fewer than a hundred entries thus far, we can say the municipalities (and contemporary villages) of Andria (Andrie) and Bitonto (Botonto) owe the most to the crown.


As mentioned above, I used the critical edition of the Catalogus Baronum by Evelyn Jamison (Jamison, Evelyn M, and Errico Cuozzo eds. Catalogus Baronum . Roma: Nella sede dell’Istituto Palazzo Borromini, 1972). I retrieved my information from the entries found within. I used the ID numbers found in the book associated with each entry to catalog my own entries, which is referred to as the ‘Book_ID’ in the CSV file. I transcribed these 99 entries by hand and did a solid amount of data cleaning. When there were lords with a series of feudi, fiefs without information specifying which fee was owed to what fief, I divided the number evenly between them. For example, if there was a lord who had three fiefs and nine knights’ fees, I divided the number evenly between them. Each fief entry received three knights’ fees owed. 


 Latin has declensions, which change the ending of nouns. I have adjusted all the names of territories and individuals so that their names appear in the nominative (the subject) case in the list. I assigned a ‘Lord_ID’ to each individual, which is different from the ‘Book_ID’. There are several lords who appear in more than one entry, and the ‘Lord_ID’ prevents individuals from appearing multiple times in the database. Collections of individuals, or those not given clear names, will have their titles, or extrapolated titles, placed in the ¨surname¨ category. 


As mentioned on the website itself, there are very few dependencies and tools. HTML is the primary coding language used, along with Python 3.12, which imports the Folium library and builds a map with Leaflet. Instructions on how one might download and run the site are found in the README in the repository. 

AI was heavily used in the making of the website. After cleaning the data and making it readable, I utilized the VSCode Copilot to generate the CSS, database, map, and it helped me organize the pages and their layouts. Beyond a few edits here and there, AI did most of the coding. AI even helped organize and write some of the instructions in the README.
